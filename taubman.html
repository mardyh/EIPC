The Taubman College of Architecture and Urban Planning, located on the University of Michigan’s North Campus, is both a home for design education and a laboratory for architectural innovation. Over the past year, EIPC has been engaged in scanning this contemporary academic space, working closely with Taubman College leadership to gain access to the studios, lecture halls, and communal spaces that define the building’s spatial experience.

Unlike historic structures composed of more opaque materials, the Taubman building posed a unique technical challenge: glass. As a material, glass is particularly problematic for LiDAR scanners, which rely on the reflection of light to capture spatial data. Because glass transmits rather than reflects light, it can result in voids, inaccuracies, or visual artifacts in the scan, complicating both registration and post-processing.

To address this, EIPC is using the Taubman building as a prototype site to test alternative workflows. This includes the integration of DSLR photogrammetry and Kinect-based depth mapping into the point cloud environment. These hybrid techniques offer new possibilities for capturing glass-heavy environments with greater fidelity, producing more accurate and legible 3D models even when traditional LiDAR scanning falters.

The scan includes key zones such as the main studio bays, the digital fabrication labs, the critique rooms, and the double-height commons, capturing not just the layout but the layered materiality of the space. Particularly notable are the glass curtain walls along the north and east façades, which present opportunities to study the relationship between transparency, structure, and surface articulation.
